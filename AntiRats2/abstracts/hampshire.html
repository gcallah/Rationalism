Intention, Intellect, and Imagination: Stuart Hampshire’s Pluralism

Kenneth B. McIntyre

Stuart Hampshire was an English philosopher associated with the group of thinkers often called ordinary language philosophers (e.g. J.L. Austin, P.F. Strawson, J.O. Urmson, et al.). Like these other philosophers, Hampshire was skeptical of the scientistic reductionism characteristic of logical positivism. In my essay, I will offer an account of the various aspects of Hampshire’s critique of enlightenment rationalism. First, I will discuss Hampshire’s sympathetic but critical appraisal of Spinoza as the enlightenment philosopher par excellence. Second I will elaborate Hampshire’s development of a pluralist epistemology which deploys a neo-Aristotelian distinction between scientific/theoretical reason and practical reason. Third, and finally, I will examine Hampshire’s value pluralism, his emphasis on the ineluctability of moral conflict, and the consequent importance of political life in his work.
