Giambattista Vico and the Imaginative Sources of Political Division

Emily B. Finley

The intensifying political polarization in America and the West has led to a radical splintering of the social fabric. It has become increasingly clear that a reassessment of the Enlightenment concept of reason, that for so long has dominated our belief about what ultimately motivates people, may be in order.

To understand the abiding political divisions in the West, highlighted in the diametrically opposed contemporary political movements of populist nationalism and democratic socialism, it is necessary to reevaluate the rationalist epistemology and to consider another epistemological framework for thinking about the limits and possibilities of politics. One of the first thinkers to oppose Cartesian rationalism, the lifeblood of Enlightenment thinking, and to offer a thoroughgoing philosophical counterargument was Giambattista Vico (1668-1744). From the perspective of Italian civic humanism, Vico argues that the imagination, rather than reason, is responsible not only for the development of culture and civilization but also our ability to know with certainty—following the project of Descartes—the nature of human existence. The considerable epistemological and philosophical insights of this eighteenth-century Neapolitan thinker can help us to understand the origins in the imagination of meaningful political division and also point toward the possibility of a more robust democratic pluralism through effective use of the imagination in the form of eloquence and wit.
