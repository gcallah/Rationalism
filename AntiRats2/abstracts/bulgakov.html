The Moral Personality of Mikhail Bulgakov

Jason Ferrell

One of the most famous Russian writers of the 20th century, Mikhail Bulgakov has been surprisingly neglected by political theorists. In this paper I attempt to rectify that by studying how he treats the issues of determinism and rationalism in his fiction, with a particular focus upon The Master and Margarita; The Heart of a Dog; and The Fatal Eggs. By exploring the allegorical elements of these works I will argue that Bulgakov dramatizes the limits of a scientific approach to politics that is ideologically driven. I will also argue that although his work is specifically written as a critique of the Soviet Union, the literary aspects of these stories causes them to transcend their original context and provide a more universal account of what the deeper springs of human behavior are.
